What This System Actually Does
Think of it like a panel of 4 independent detectives, each examining the same video through a completely different lens:
Detective 1 — rPPG (Physiological): Looks for a heartbeat signal in the face. Real human skin subtly changes color with every pulse of blood. Deepfake-generated faces either have no heartbeat signal or an incoherent one. This runs on 9 facial regions using MediaPipe, extracts 117 features (heart rate, frequency spectrum, cross-region consistency), and feeds them to an XGBoost/LightGBM stack.
Detective 2 — EfficientNet-B4: A visual pattern expert. Looks at 16 face frames and detects spatial artifacts — blending seams, texture inconsistencies, unnatural skin — that deepfake generators leave behind.
Detective 3 — Xception + Frequency: Same visual job but also looks in the frequency domain (DCT/FFT). Compression artifacts from deepfake encoders show up as specific frequency signatures invisible to the naked eye.
Detective 4 — Swin Transformer: Uses shifted-window attention to capture long-range relationships across the face, plus a DCT branch. More global than Xception.
The Ensemble: Each detective outputs a P(fake) between 0 and 1. The Optuna-tuned weights [rPPG=0.11, EfficientNet=0.34, Swin=0.55] combine them into one final score. The reason this beats any single model is that they catch different kinds of fakes — a deepfake that fools the visual detectives might still have no heartbeat signal, and vice versa.
Is this the right approach? Yes — this is exactly how serious deepfake detection research is done. The AUC of 0.808 vs 0.788 for the best single model validates that the diversity is real.
